# Formulas: Structural Maps and When to Use Them

Formulas are **structural maps of the decision process**, not magic. They organize the argument; they cannot save a message aimed at the wrong awareness stage (diagnose first: see `diagnostics.md`). Choose by awareness stage, audience temperature, length, and offer complexity. And **hybridize freely.** A long sales page routinely runs one formula to open, another in the body, and a third to close.

Caveat worth holding in mind: the academic record (Vakratsas & Ambler's review of 250+ studies, plus later neuroscience critiques of AIDA) finds **no universal fixed sequence** of advertising effects. Attention, interest, desire, and action fire in parallel, not tidy order. So treat every formula below as a flexible thinking tool, not a law.

---

## Quick selector

| Situation | Reach for |
|---|---|
| Cold, low-awareness, needs to feel the problem first | Star-Story-Solution, PASTOR, 12-step VSL |
| Problem-aware, in acute pain, short-form | **PAS** |
| Improvement-motivated (not in acute pain), mid-funnel | **BAB** |
| Solution/Product-aware, weighing options | **AIDA**, **4 Ps**, **FAB** |
| Cold audience needing education before persuasion | **QUEST** |
| Most-aware, ready to buy | Lead with the offer / **the Stack** (see `offers.md`) |
| Grading a single headline | **4 U's** checklist |

---

## The structures

**AIDA: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action.** The grandfather (E. St. Elmo Lewis, 1898). Best for longer sequences and full landing pages; mirrors the funnel. *Common failure:* writers nail Attention-Interest-Action and skip the **Desire** build, the part that actually does the selling. Don't shortcut Desire.

**PAS: Problem → Agitate → Solution.** Called the most reliable formula ever written. Best for **problem-aware** audiences, short-form, ads, emails, cold-but-hurting traffic. The agitation is what creates urgency: name the pain, twist it (honestly) until the reader *needs* relief, then resolve.

**PASTOR: Problem → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation → Offer → Response.** PAS's long-form, warmer cousin. Best for values-driven audiences and long sales pages; story-led, identity-aware. The Transformation step paints the after-state; the Offer and Response steps close.

**BAB: Before → After → Bridge.** "Here's your world now; here's the after; here's the bridge that gets you there." Best when the audience is *improvement-motivated* rather than in acute pain. Great for mid-funnel emails. Lighter and more aspirational than PAS.

**The 4 Ps: Promise → Picture → Proof → Push.** Strong for ads and short landing pages. *Picture* does the dimensionalizing (the vivid end-state), *Proof* does the credibility work, *Push* is the CTA + urgency. A compact, complete arc.

**The 4 U's: Urgent, Unique, Useful, Ultra-specific.** Not a body structure: a **headline checklist.** Grade every headline candidate against all four. A great headline usually hits at least three; the weakest candidates miss "ultra-specific" first.

**FAB: Features → Advantages → Benefits.** The translation engine that lives *inside* other formulas' bodies. Turns a product fact into reader payoff: feature (what it is) → advantage (what it does) → benefit (what that means for me). Push every benefit further into a *dimensionalized* end-state (see `mechanics.md`).

**QUEST: Qualify → Understand → Educate → Stimulate → Transition.** Good for cold audiences that need education before persuasion. *Qualify* signals who this is for; *Understand* proves empathy; *Educate* reframes; *Stimulate* builds desire; *Transition* moves to the offer.

**Star-Story-Solution.** A hero (the Star, often a relatable protagonist or the product itself) goes through a Story that resolves with your Solution. The narrative spine behind Caples' famous "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano…" ad and behind most VSLs. Best for cold, low-awareness traffic that needs to be pulled in before it's sold.

**The 12-step VSL spine.** The long-form direct-response workhorse for cold-to-warm traffic: hook → big promise → intro → problem → agitation/villain → mechanism reveal → credibility → benefit stacking → social proof → offer & bonuses → urgency → close/CTA. Use as a checklist for video sales letters and long sales pages; not every step needs equal weight, but a missing step is usually a visible hole.

## Hybridization in practice

Real assets blend structures by section:

- **Open** with PAS to capture the problem-aware reader fast.
- **Body** in FAB / 4 Ps for logic and proof.
- **Close** with AIDA's Desire→Action or the Stack for the offer.

Tendencies (not rules): **B2C** leans emotional (AIDA, BAB, story); **B2B** leans risk/ROI/logic (PAS, 4 Ps, VSL), but still sandwiches emotion around the logic, because B2B buyers are people justifying a decision they made emotionally. When in doubt, let the **awareness stage** pick the opener and the **offer complexity** pick the closer.
