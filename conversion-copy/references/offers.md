# Offers: Engineering What You're Actually Selling

The offer is often higher-leverage than the prose. Hormozi's hierarchy: **market > offer > persuasion.** You can't out-write a weak offer to a wrong market, but a genuinely great offer to the right market sells through mediocre copy. So when copy isn't converting and the headline/lead is sound, look here next: the lever is usually the offer, not the adjectives.

Reach for this file when you're constructing or critiquing an offer.

---

## The Value Equation (Hormozi)

> **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

Maximize the top two, minimize the bottom two. As the denominator approaches zero, value approaches infinity. Use it as a diagnostic on any offer:

- **Dream outcome**: is the promised end-state big enough, and is it framed as the *status shift* the buyer actually wants?
- **Perceived likelihood**: does proof, guarantee, and mechanism make success *believable for them specifically*?
- **Time delay**: how fast do they get a first win? Shorten it, or add a quick early payoff.
- **Effort & sacrifice**: what must they do/give up? Strip it, or do it for them.

Most weak offers fail on the bottom two: the buyer believes the outcome but dreads the time and effort. Attack those.

## Building the offer: Grand Slam / Godfather

Both Hormozi's "Grand Slam Offer" and Suby's "Godfather Offer" describe an offer so good the prospect feels stupid saying no. The build:

1. **Identify the dream outcome** (the status-laden end-state).
2. **List every obstacle / objection** between the prospect and that outcome.
3. **Reverse each obstacle into a solution**: each becomes a component of the offer.
4. **Choose delivery vehicles** for each solution (how it's delivered).
5. **Trim and stack** into a bundle, then price it far below its stated/created value.

Suby's seven Godfather parts are a useful checklist for the finished offer: rationale, value build, pricing, payment options, premiums/bonuses, power guarantee, scarcity.

## The Stack (Brunson)

Reveal the **full stacked offer on one "slide"** (every component with its individual value) *before* you reveal the price. This anchors the price against the *whole stack*, not the last item mentioned. The reader should be adding it up in their head and bracing for a big number before you undercut it.

## Pricing and anchoring

- **Anchor** the price against the stacked total, or against a costlier alternative the reader already knows.
- **Justify** the price with an explicit rationale: an unexplained number invites resistance; a reason for it ("priced for volume," "less than one service call") disarms it.
- **Add bonuses, don't discount the core.** Discounting cheapens the perceived value of the main thing; bonuses raise total value while protecting the anchor. Make bonuses address the *next* objection or the *next* desire after purchase.

## Guarantees: risk reversal is the keystone

The guarantee reverses the risk from buyer to seller and is frequently the single highest-converting element. Four types (Hormozi); Sugarman's "satisfaction conviction" and Suby's "power guarantee" are the same instinct:

1. **Unconditional**: "money back, no questions, any reason." Strongest trust signal.
2. **Conditional**: money back *if* they did X and it didn't work; doubles as a commitment device.
3. **Anti-guarantee**: "all sales final," used deliberately to signal exclusivity/seriousness in specific markets.
4. **Implied / performance**: you only win when they win (e.g., pay-on-results).

Pick the strongest guarantee you can honestly stand behind. A bold guarantee also *forces* a better product, which is the point.

## Urgency and scarcity: honest only

Real deadlines and real limits, disclosed plainly, are legitimate and effective (Cialdini's scarcity; loss aversion). **Fabricated** urgency (fake countdowns, false "last chance," invented stock counts) converts in the short term and erodes trust (and invites legal/compliance exposure in regulated verticals) in the long term. Default to honesty; Sugarman ranks it the #1 trigger. If there is no real scarcity, create a *real* one (a genuine cohort cap, a true price-rise date, a real bonus deadline) rather than faking it.

## The two pressure-tests before you ship an offer

1. **Hormozi's Value Equation**: does it maximize outcome and likelihood while minimizing time and effort?
2. **Suby's question**: *"Is this offer so good it would keep you up at night?"* If not, it isn't finished. Add value (don't cut price) until it is.
