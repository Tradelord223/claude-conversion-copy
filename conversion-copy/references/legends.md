# The Canon: Legends → Reusable Principles

Read this for background, for the *why* behind a technique, or to deliberately channel one master's approach ("write this the way Halbert would"). Each entry compresses a legend's signature contribution into a principle you can actually reuse. The caveats at the end matter as much as the techniques; they keep the skill honest.

---

## The legends

**Claude Hopkins** (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923): father of measurable, test-driven advertising. **Reason-why copy** (give concrete reasons to buy), the **preemptive claim** (seize an obvious product attribute every competitor could claim but none has bothered to: his Schlitz "our bottles are washed with live steam" lifted the brand from fifth to a tie for first), **keyed coupons** (he invented them to track what actually pulled), and **sampling** (make the prospect ask, so they're pre-committed). Doctrine: "Advertising is salesmanship… The only purpose of advertising is to make sales."
→ *Principle:* Test everything; claim the specific demonstrable thing first; treat copy as measurable salesmanship, never art.

**David Ogilvy** (*Confessions*; *Ogilvy on Advertising*) bridged brand image and direct response. The **headline is ~80 cents of the dollar** (five times as many read the headline as the body); **research before writing** (three weeks in Rolls-Royce engineering docs produced "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise… is the electric clock"); **respect the reader** ("The consumer is not a moron; she is your wife"); **long copy sells** when stakes are high; **brand image** as a long-term asset (the Hathaway eyepatch's "story appeal").
→ *Principle:* Do the homework, lead with a benefit/news headline, tell the truth made fascinating, never talk down.

**Gary Halbert** (*The Boron Letters*): the most visceral direct-mail mind. **A-pile/B-pile** (people sort mail over a trash can; look like personal A-pile: transfers straight to inboxes and feeds); **"a starving crowd"** (market first: you'd win at selling burgers with one advantage: a hungry crowd); **read it aloud** to find friction; **grabbers** (physical attention-seizers). "Truth is determined not by how people use their mouths, but by how they use their wallets."
→ *Principle:* Win the attention-sort by looking human; pick a proven hungry market; make copy so smooth it can't be stumbled over.

**Eugene Schwartz** (*Breakthrough Advertising*, 1966): the most important strategic mind in copy. The **5 Stages of Awareness** and **5 Stages of Market Sophistication** (see `diagnostics.md`), plus **mass desire** (channel desire, don't create it) and the rule that copy must carry "emotion, image, logic and promise."
→ *Principle:* Before writing a word, locate the prospect on two axes (how much they know, how many claims they've heard) and let that dictate everything.

**Joseph Sugarman** (*Adweek Copywriting Handbook*; *Triggers*): flow-and-psychology master. The **slippery slide** (every element exists to get the first sentence read; every sentence to get the next read); the **first sentence short, almost incomplete**; **seeds of curiosity**; ~30 **psychological triggers** with **honesty** as the most powerful, plus involvement/ownership, greed, authority, and **satisfaction conviction** (a guarantee strong enough to push the prospect over the edge).
→ *Principle:* Engineer relentless forward motion; treat the first sentence as the only goal of everything above it; layer honest triggers.

**Robert Collier** (*The Robert Collier Letter Book*) originated the most-quoted idea in copy: **"Always enter the conversation already taking place in the customer's mind."** Also: the mind thinks in pictures; build a vivid word-picture the reader colors in.
→ *Principle:* Start where the reader already is, in the language and emotion already in their head.

**John Caples** (*Tested Advertising Methods*): headline-testing pioneer. His "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano, But When I Started to Play!" (1926) is among the most imitated ads ever: story + curiosity loop + social transformation. Findings: the headline is **50 to 90% of the effect**; lead with **self-interest, news, or curiosity** (ideally combined); **be specific**; trust tests over opinion.
→ *Principle:* Win on the headline; lead with benefit/news/curiosity; get specific; let data decide.

**Rosser Reeves** (*Reality in Advertising*, 1961) coined the **USP**: (1) make a proposition: "buy this, get this specific benefit"; (2) one the competition can't or doesn't offer; (3) strong enough to pull new customers. (M&M's "melts in your mouth, not in your hand.")
→ *Principle:* Isolate the one benefit only you can claim, and hammer it. Modern evolution: the **Unique Mechanism**, a distinctive *how* that revives a stale claim.

**Robert Cialdini** (*Influence*; *Pre-Suasion*). The **7 principles**: Reciprocity, Commitment & Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity, Unity. (Detailed in `mechanics.md`.)
→ *Principle:* Bake the seven triggers in deliberately and ethically. They're levers on decisions the reader is already inclined to make.

**Drew Eric Whitman** (*Cashvertising*) codified the **Life-Force 8**, the biological desires behind more sales than all other wants combined (see `mechanics.md`), plus the "quick, easy, cheap" appeals and the fear-then-solution structure.
→ *Principle:* Tie the offer to one or more biological LF8 drives; biology outsells learned wants.

**Alex Hormozi** (*$100M Offers*): modern offer architecture. The **Value Equation**, the **Grand Slam Offer**, demand enhancers (scarcity, urgency, bonuses; never discount the core), and four guarantee types (see `offers.md`). Hierarchy: **market > offer > persuasion.**
→ *Principle:* Engineer the offer itself to maximize perceived value and minimize cost/time/effort; make saying no feel stupid.

**Sabri Suby** (*Sell Like Crazy*): the **Godfather Offer**; the **Halo Strategy** (deep dream-buyer research before writing); **HVCO + Magic Lantern** nurture; the **Larger Market Formula** (only ~3% buy now; serve the other 97%: 17% gathering info, 20% problem-aware, 60% unaware). "A prescription without a diagnosis is malpractice."
→ *Principle:* Diagnose like a doctor, build an offer that keeps you up at night, serve the whole market, not just the ready sliver.

**Russell Brunson** (*DotCom Secrets*; *Expert Secrets*): **Hook-Story-Offer** (a failing funnel is always one of the three); the **Epiphany Bridge** (tell the emotional origin of your own "aha" so the prospect has it too: lead with emotion, let them justify with logic after); the **Big Domino** (one belief that collapses all objections); the **Stack**.
→ *Principle:* Wrap the offer in an emotional epiphany story; knock down the one belief that matters; stack value visibly before price.

**Donald Miller** (*Building a StoryBrand*, SB7): **customer-as-hero, brand-as-guide.** Seven elements: a Character with a Problem (external, internal, AND philosophical) meets a Guide (empathy + authority) who gives a Plan and a Call to Action, helping them avoid Failure and reach Success. "If you confuse, you lose." The grunt test: can a stranger grasp what you offer, how it helps, and how to buy in 5 seconds?
→ *Principle:* Never make your brand the hero. Name the internal and philosophical problem, not just the external one.

**Joanna Wiebe / Copyhackers** pioneered **Voice-of-Customer / message mining**: the best copy is found, not written (see `mechanics.md`). Process: research → write → validate; judge by conversion, not applause.
→ *Principle:* Don't invent claims. Harvest the market's own language and emotional phrasing, then test it.

**Ann Handley** (*Everybody Writes*): modern editorial craft. The **Ugly First Draft** ("show up and throw up," then edit brilliantly); **empathy as a discipline** ("Don't tell me who you are. Tell me why you matter to me"); content quality = utility × inspiration × empathy; **show don't tell**; readability; write to an audience of one; ask "So what?" relentlessly.
→ *Principle:* Lower the bar to start (ugly draft), raise it to finish (ruthless editing); lead with empathy and utility.

**The prose-craft canon** (Strunk & White, Zinsser, Orwell, Provost, King, Clark, Klinkenborg): the lineage behind "sound human." **Provost**: vary sentence length to make music ("Don't just write words. Write music"), which is the rhythm half of `mechanics.md`'s sound-human pass. **Strunk & White / Zinsser**: omit needless words, prefer the specific and concrete to the vague and abstract ("A period of unfavorable weather set in" → "It rained every day for a week"), cut the long word for the short. **Orwell** (six rules): never use a metaphor you're used to seeing in print, never a long word where a short one does, cut any word you can, prefer the active, drop jargon, and break any rule before writing something barbarous. **King**: "the road to hell is paved with adverbs"; trust the strong verb. **Clark**: dig for the concrete (the brand of the beer), seek original images, put the strong word at the period. **Klinkenborg**: distrust the scaffolding (mechanical transitions, topic-sentence-then-dutiful-support, conclusions that restate), the very habits that make prose read as machine-assembled.
→ *Principle:* Voice, concrete specifics, and varied rhythm are what make copy read as written by a person to a person. This is the same craft direct-response always demanded, now also the antidote to template-flavored prose; pursue it by writing better, never by gaming a tell.

## Caveats: what keeps this skill honest

- **Formula skepticism is warranted.** Vakratsas & Ambler, "How Advertising Works: What Do We Really Know?" (*Journal of Marketing*, 1999), a review of 250+ studies, plus later neuroscience critiques of AIDA find **no universal fixed sequence** of effects; attention, interest, desire, and action fire in parallel. Treat formulas as flexible thinking tools, not laws.
- **Source-quality notes.** Much practitioner detail (Halbert, Sugarman, Schwartz, Hormozi, Suby, Brunson) is best documented in the authors' own books and consistent secondary summaries, not peer-reviewed sources. The *frameworks* are reliably reported; specific vendor performance numbers ("400%+ lift," big revenue figures) are self-reported marketing claims. Use them as illustrative, not proof. The Zeigarnik "waiter" story is the popularized version of her 1927 work.
- **The ethical line on scarcity/urgency.** The greats distinguish *real* scarcity/urgency (genuine deadlines, true limits, honest disclosure) from manufactured pressure (fake countdowns, false "last chance"). Fabricated urgency converts short-term and erodes trust (and creates compliance exposure in regulated verticals) long-term. Default to honesty.
- **"Sound human" means write better, not game a detector.** The sound-human pass in `mechanics.md` exists because template-flavored copy doesn't convert, not to beat AI-detection software. Those detectors are documented to be unreliable and biased (Liang et al., *Patterns* 2023, found an average ~61% false-positive rate on non-native-English writing; OpenAI discontinued its own classifier for low accuracy), so a detector score is never the bar. The bar is: does the copy read aloud like a sharp person wrote it, and is the craft done? If genuinely strong, voice-rich copy still trips a detector, that's the detector's failure, not a reason to degrade the writing. And the legitimate use is a human improving real copy, editing an AI-assisted draft to actually sound like them, rescuing robotic prose, never passing off machine work where honesty is owed.
- **Brand vs. direct-response tension is real but reconcilable.** Pure direct-response optimizes the next click; pure brand builds long-term equity. Use the sophistication stage to set the blend: lean direct-response in early/result-driven markets, lean brand/identity/story in saturated ones, and remember a single asset often must do both at once.
