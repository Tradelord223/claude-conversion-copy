# Mechanics: The Transferable Moves

These are the craft-level techniques that work inside any structure, at any awareness stage. They are *how* the copy actually does its job sentence by sentence. Reach for this file when you're writing or editing the body.

## The Big Idea: one unifying hook

Ogilvy: *"It takes a big idea to attract the attention of consumers and get them to buy your product. Unless your advertising contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night."* Enforce **one dominant idea per message**: one message developed well beats an ad overloaded with competing ideas.

**Ogilvy's five tests for a big idea:** (1) Did it make you gasp when you first saw it? (2) Do you wish you'd thought of it yourself? (3) Is it unique? (4) Does it fit the strategy to perfection? (5) Could it be used for 30 years? Big ideas come from a *well-informed* unconscious. Saturate yourself with research, then let it surface. If your idea isn't unique and doesn't make you slightly gasp, keep digging.

## Voice of Customer (VoC): the research that feeds everything

The best copy is **found, not written** (Joanna Wiebe). Harvest the market's exact words from reviews, forums, Reddit, support tickets, and sales-call transcripts, then lift the real pains, desired outcomes, and emotional phrasing straight into the copy. This operationalizes Collier's rule (*"always enter the conversation already taking place in the customer's mind"*) and the homework-first ethic shared by Hopkins, Ogilvy, and Halbert.

The canonical proof: for the Beachway rehab center, the control headline "Your addiction ends here" was replaced with a line mined from a book review: **"If you think you need rehab, you do."** Per Copyblogger's account of the test, the mined variation drove **400%+ more clicks** and 26% more completed lead submissions. The lesson is not the number (treat it as illustrative) but the method: *don't invent claims; harvest the market's own language, then test it.*

When you have no source material, say so, write to the most probable language, and flag where real VoC would sharpen the copy. Never fabricate a quote or a statistic to fill the gap.

## Features → Benefits → Dimensionalized benefits

- A **feature** is what it *is* ("aircraft-grade aluminum body").
- A **benefit** is what it *does for me* ("survives drops without denting").
- A **dimensionalized benefit** paints the *felt end-state* ("the one tool in the drawer that still looks new after five years on the job site, and the only one your crew keeps borrowing").

Always push past the benefit to the vivid, sensory, status-laden picture of life *after* the purchase (Collier's "word picture," Handley's "show, don't tell," StoryBrand's "Success" vision, Hormozi's "golf buddies' jaws drop"). Status is the deep currency: sell *who the buyer becomes*, not just what the product does.

## The curiosity engine: what creates forward motion

The reader's default is to stop reading. These mechanics defeat that default.

- **Zeigarnik effect** (Bluma Zeigarnik, 1920s): the mind fixates on *unfinished* tasks and questions. Waiters recalled unpaid orders but forgot them the moment they were paid. Open a loop and the reader can't fully rest until it closes.
- **Open loop:** introduce an unresolved question or story early, resolve it late. Rule: **pay off every loop you open**, and don't open so many that the reader loses track. ("She finally understood why he left, and wished she didn't.")
- **Curiosity gap** (Loewenstein's information-gap theory): reveal ~70% so the brain races to fill the last 30%. If the gap is too *wide*, the reader can't care; if too narrow, there's nothing to chase.
- **Bucket brigades:** short, colon-capped bridge phrases that kill the urge to bounce: "Here's the deal:", "But it gets better:", "Want to know the best part?", "What does this mean for you?", "Now:", "But here's the kicker:". Drop one wherever a reader might hit the back button. (An old sales-letter tactic; Brian Dean adapted it for web/SEO content.)
- **Sugarman's slippery slide** is the macro version of all of the above: *every element of an ad exists to get the first sentence read, and every sentence exists to get the next one read.* Make the first sentence short and almost incomplete so reading is easier than skipping. End sections with "seeds of curiosity" ("But there's more," "So read on," "I'll explain in a minute").

## Specificity and concreteness

Specific numbers beat round ones; sensory detail beats abstraction; demonstration beats assertion. **Specificity is itself proof**: it activates belief where generality activates skepticism. ("Lose 5 pounds in 7 days" beats "lose weight fast.") Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce headline ("At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock") was *excavated from an engineering report*, not invented. Hopkins, Caples, Ogilvy, and Whitman all converge here: the concrete claim outsells the superlative.

## Emotional drivers: the levers people actually pull

People buy on emotion and identity, then justify with logic. Three catalogs of motivation, all pointing the same way:

**Whitman's Life-Force 8 (LF8)**, biologically hardwired desires, responsible for more sales than all other wants combined: (1) survival / life-extension, (2) enjoyment of food & drink, (3) freedom from fear, pain, and danger, (4) sexual companionship, (5) comfortable living conditions, (6) to be superior / winning / keeping up with the Joneses, (7) care and protection of loved ones, (8) social approval. Tie the offer to one or more LF8 drives. Biology is the most bankable motivator. (Secondary, *learned* wants (saving money, convenience, quality) are weaker; prefer the biological eight.)

**Cialdini's 7 principles of ethical persuasion**, the social substrate beneath every offer and CTA: **Reciprocity** (give first), **Commitment & Consistency** (get a small aligned yes), **Social Proof** (people copy others under uncertainty), **Authority** (show credentials/results), **Liking** (we say yes to those we like and resemble), **Scarcity** (rare = valued; loss aversion), and **Unity** (shared identity / "we-ness," the strongest and newest). Bake these in deliberately and honestly.

**Status** (Hormozi): the deep currency of "dream outcome." Most aspirations are really about standing in someone's eyes. When you name the dream, name the status shift inside it.

## Objection handling and pre-emption

Address the reader's skepticism *before* they voice it. List every objection the prospect could raise, then reverse each into a line of copy. Techniques: Brunson's **Big Domino** (find the one core belief that, if accepted, collapses every objection) and false-belief breaking; Sugarman's **honesty trigger** (raise the product's flaw yourself: admitting the small weakness earns trust for the big claim); Whitman's direct competitor comparison. A pre-empted objection is far more persuasive than a defended one.

## Proof elements

Belief is built, not asserted. The toolkit: **social proof** (testimonials, counts, "join 8,000+…"), **specificity** (see above), **demonstrations**, **authority/credibility signals**, and **risk reversal via guarantees** (see `offers.md`). Gary Bencivenga's rule governs all of them: **never make a claim bigger than your proof.** If the proof is thin, shrink the claim until it's covered. A smaller believed claim outsells a bigger doubted one.

## Voice, tone, rhythm, readability

The mechanics that make direct response *slide* also make brand/editorial writing a pleasure to read:

- **Vary sentence length deliberately, this is the music.** One idea per sentence, but mix short, medium, and long so the rhythm never drones. Gary Provost's demonstration says it best: five-word sentences are fine, "but several together become monotonous… The ear demands some variety." Use short punches for emphasis, then occasionally release a longer, building sentence that carries energy. Uniform sentence length is the single flattest tell that copy was machine-assembled; real human rhythm clusters short and long. **Don't just write words. Write music.**
- **Em dashes: rare and deliberate.** Stacked em dashes are the loudest punctuation signal that a machine wrote the copy, and they flatten rhythm into a monotone. Hold to about one per paragraph at most. Replace the rest with a period, a comma, a colon, or parentheses, or rewrite the sentence so the mark is not needed. The em dash is a real rhythm tool, so don't ban it blindly; when you cut one, preserve the beat it was carrying. On the final pass, hunt them down: every em dash must earn its place or get cut.
- Write at a low reading grade; sentences mostly under ~25 words; short paragraphs; use whitespace.
- Use **"you"** relentlessly; write to an audience of *one*.
- **Read it aloud** to find friction: wherever you stumble speaking it, the reader stumbles reading it (Halbert). The ear catches droning uniformity and anything you'd never actually say out loud, things the eye skates right over.
- Cut jargon and "Frankenwords" (Handley); cut the warm-up first paragraph; conversational beats corporate.
- Strong **lede** and **kicker**: "a good lede invites you to the party and a good kicker makes you wish you could stay longer."
- Ask **"So what?"** after every claim until you reach something the reader actually cares about.
- Draft ugly, edit ruthlessly (Handley's "show up and throw up," then become an excellent editor of your own work). Quality content = utility × inspiration × empathy; if any factor is zero, the product is zero.

## Sound human: cut the machine tells

This is an evolution of the same standard the greats always held (Halbert's "read it aloud," Ogilvy's "the consumer is not a moron"), now sharpened for an era when most flat copy reads as machine-generated. The point isn't to dodge an AI detector; it's that prose carrying these tells reads as generic, committee-written, and untrustworthy, and generic copy doesn't convert. Specific, opinionated, human-sounding copy does. Run this as a dedicated pass after the body is drafted.

**Why it works (and why it's the same craft, not a new trick):** good persuasive writing is, almost by definition, less predictable and more varied than autopilot prose. Genuine voice, concrete specifics, and real sentence rhythm are exactly what make copy feel like a person wrote it to *you*. So the moves below are the same ones Orwell, Zinsser, and Provost prescribed; they just double as the fix for copy that reads like a template.

**Lexical tells, replace with how you'd actually say it.** Cut or swap the autopilot vocabulary that has become a uniform of machine prose: *delve, tapestry, underscore, pivotal, showcase, intricate, foster, garner, vibrant, boasts, bolster, crucial, meticulous, testament, realm, landscape, navigate the landscape, leverage, robust, seamless, comprehensive, elevate, unlock, supercharge, game-changer.* Restore plain verbs: "delve into" → "dig into"; "boasts/features" → "has"; "serves as" → "is." Phrase-level offenders to delete outright: "it's important to note," "it is worth noting," "in today's fast-paced world," "in the ever-evolving landscape of," "stands as a testament to," "plays a vital role." Just state the thing.

**Structural tells, the ones that quietly scream "template":**
- **"It's not just X, it's Y."** The single most reliable tell. State the point directly and affirmatively instead of through negation.
- **The reflexive rule of three.** "Innovative, transformative, and groundbreaking." If the third item only completes the rhythm rather than adding something, cut it. (Three used *deliberately* for impact is fine; the reflex is the problem.)
- **"From X to Y" false ranges.** "From scenic landscapes to historic landmarks…" Name the actual specifics without the frame.
- **Superficial "-ing" tails.** "…highlighting the need for innovation," "…underscoring its significance," "…reflecting broader trends." Delete the pseudo-analytic tail; it adds nothing.
- **Tidy wrap-ups.** "In conclusion," "Overall," "In summary," and conclusions that restate what was already said. End on a concrete, forward image, not a recap. (This pairs with the **kicker**: leave them wanting to stay, don't tuck them in.)
- **Significance inflation.** "Leaves a lasting impact," "a watershed moment," "plays a significant role." Show the specific fact and let the significance be inferred, which is also just `## Specificity and concreteness` applied.
- **Vague attribution / false balance.** "Experts say," "studies suggest," "some argue." Name the real source or take the real position. Conversion copy especially cannot afford to sound non-committal; pick a stance.
- **The bolded-term-plus-colon list reflex.** Write prose unless a list genuinely helps scanning.

**Formatting tells:** title-case headings (use sentence case), bold on every other term (bold only what truly needs emphasis), decorative emoji, and any leftover markdown or chatbot artifacts ("Certainly!", "Here's your copy on…"). Strip them.

This pass is **subtractive and craft-driven only.** Never reach for the quality-destroying "humanizer" tricks, deliberate typos, grammar errors, invisible Unicode, thesaurus-swapping, weird-word padding. They wreck readability (the opposite of everything above), increasingly fail anyway, and have no place in copy meant to sell. The honest, durable way to sound human is to *write* better: real voice, real specifics, real rhythm.
