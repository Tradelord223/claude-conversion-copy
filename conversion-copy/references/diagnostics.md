# Diagnostics: The Two Axes That Set Your Angle

This is the most important reference in the skill. Before you choose a structure, write a headline, or build an offer, you locate the prospect on two independent axes. Together they determine your lead, your claim-framing, and how long you wait before mentioning the product. Get these wrong and nothing downstream can save the copy; get them right and even plain prose converts.

The two axes are independent. **Awareness** is about the *prospect's* knowledge. **Sophistication** is about the *market's* history of claims. You need both: a saturated, jaded market of unaware prospects calls for an identity-led story; an early market of ready buyers calls for a blunt offer.

---

## Axis A: The 5 Stages of Awareness (Eugene Schwartz)

*How much does the prospect already know?* This sets your lead and the moment you introduce the product. The colder the traffic, the lower the awareness, the more education and emotion you need before the pitch.

**1. Unaware:** doesn't know they have the problem, or won't admit it.
- *Audience:* largest, coldest, longest path to sale.
- *Approach:* don't pitch. Tell a story, use an indirect or identity-based lead, news, or provocation to make the latent desire conscious. The reader must feel the problem before they'll care about a solution. (Volkswagen selling small cars into a big-car market.)

**2. Problem-Aware:** feels the pain, doesn't know solutions exist.
- *Approach:* lead with the problem. Agitate it, then *validate* the frustration so the reader feels understood ("yes, that's exactly it"). Introduce the *category* of solution near the end, not the top. PAS is built for this stage.

**3. Solution-Aware:** knows solution types exist, hasn't picked one, doesn't know your product.
- *Approach:* lead with the desired result/benefit, then position your *type* of solution as the best route to it. Differentiate from the alternatives the reader is weighing.

**4. Product-Aware:** knows your product, isn't yet convinced.
- *Approach:* specifics, features→benefits, head-to-head comparisons, proof, testimonials. This is where urgency belongs. They're close; give them the reason and the nudge.

**5. Most-Aware:** ready; just needs the deal or the push.
- *Audience:* smallest, warmest, most competitive, most expensive traffic.
- *Approach:* lead with the offer: price, bonus, deadline, and exactly how to buy. Don't re-sell what they already believe; remove friction and close.

### The decision rule
**Match the lead to the stage.** A product-demo lead shown to a problem-aware audience fails not because it's a bad demo but because it's pitched *above* the reader's stage. Cold / top-of-funnel = lower awareness = more story and emotion. Warm / bottom-of-funnel = higher awareness = benefit, proof, offer.

When you can't tell, **assume lower awareness.** Educating a reader who was actually ready wastes a little of their time; pitching a reader who wasn't ready loses them entirely. The asymmetry favors caution.

### Modern variant: UPSYD
A useful relabeling for funnels: **U**naware → **P**roblem-aware → **S**olution-aware → **Y**ou-aware (knows your brand) → **D**eal-aware (knows your offer). Same ladder, marketing-friendly names.

## Axis B: The 5 Stages of Market Sophistication (Eugene Schwartz)

*How many similar claims has this market already heard?* This sets how you must *frame* the claim. The same true promise has to be packaged differently depending on how worn-out the market is.

**Stage 1: first to market.** No one has made this claim. Just *name* it, plainly. ("Lose weight.") Simplicity wins; mechanism would only get in the way.

**Stage 2: a competitor appears.** Enlarge / outbid the claim. ("Lose weight in 30 days.") Bigger, faster, more.

**Stage 3: the market is jaded with claims.** Bare promises no longer land. Introduce a **mechanism**: a *how* that makes the tired promise believable again. ("Lose weight by resetting the hormone that controls fat storage.") This is the birth of the **Unique Mechanism**, and most contested markets live here.

**Stage 4: mechanisms compete.** Everyone now has a mechanism. Enlarge or elaborate yours (make it bigger, cleaner, more elegant) or discover a genuinely new one.

**Stage 5: the market is exhausted; nothing new is believed.** The reader is actively looking for reasons to *exclude* you. Stop competing on claim or mechanism and pivot to **identity, brand, emotion, and experience.** You win on *who you are* and *who your customer becomes.* (Fashion, cars, phones: the "iconic" brands.)

### The decision rule
**Count the competing claims your audience has already heard.** Market a Stage-4 audience with Stage-2 tactics (a bare, enlarged claim) and you'll be ignored as more noise. Conversely, burying a Stage-1 market under elaborate mechanism is wasted effort. They just need the plain claim.

---

## Combining the two axes

Awareness × Sophistication together set the entire angle. Read them as a grid:

- **Low awareness + high sophistication** (the hardest, most common hard case): the reader doesn't yet feel the problem *and* has heard every claim in the category. Lead with **story / identity** to surface the desire, and carry a **mechanism** to earn belief once you pitch. Long-form, emotion-forward.
- **High awareness + low sophistication:** they want it and haven't been pitched to death. **Lead with the offer**, state the claim plainly, close fast.
- **High awareness + high sophistication:** ready buyers in a jaded market. Differentiate on **mechanism and proof**, then offer.
- **Low awareness + low sophistication:** rare and enviable (a real new problem, a fresh market). **Educate simply**, name the problem and the plain solution.

If you take nothing else from this file: **diagnose both axes before you write, and when in doubt, assume the harder case** (lower awareness, higher sophistication) and lead with story plus mechanism. It's the robust default.
